# Competitor Deep Dive — [SaaS Incumbent A]

**Type:** Example, blurred. Modelled on a real competitor deep dive.
**Threat rating:** MODERATE
**Last updated:** 2026-06

---

## Summary

The most-cited alternative in our category. Well-funded, established brand, aggressive sales team. Wins on breadth, loses on workflow fit. We compete on the workflow-fit axis and let them own the buyers who want a big-brand horizontal solution.

---

## What they sell

- **Product:** A horizontal SaaS CRM for contractors. Estimating, project management, CRM, some marketing tools.
- **Pricing:** Public, tiered. Starting around $199/mo, going up to $499/mo depending on features and seats.
- **Model:** Monthly SaaS, no code ownership, integrations with third-party vendor tools.

## Sales motion

- **Inbound funnel** — paid social, SEO on high-intent contractor terms.
- **Book-a-demo** as the primary CTA.
- **Trained sales team** on demo calls. Discovery + demo + follow-up + close, typical 2-3 week sales cycle.
- **Content marketing** — a blog + a podcast + a YouTube channel. Consistent output.
- **Free trial** — 14 days.

## Product surface

- Deep feature set. Estimating engine, project scheduling, invoicing, subcontractor management, some AI features layered on top.
- Mobile apps (iOS + Android). Field-usable.
- Integrations library — QuickBooks, standard vendor tools, some carrier APIs.

## Their pricing story

- They are on the record that "contractors want simple pricing." Their tiers reflect that — 3 named tiers, no per-seat mid-tier gimmicks.
- Pricing is above what a $500k-$1M contractor typically wants to pay for a horizontal tool but below what a $5M+ contractor considers material.

## Where they win

- **Buyers who don't yet have workflow conviction.** A first-time CRM shopper who doesn't know they'll want to bend the tool.
- **Buyers who value brand.** Recognized name matters to some, especially those who talk to their bank about tech investments.
- **Buyers who need the integrations library.** If they're already using 4 tools that all integrate, switching to a custom build means giving up those integrations.
- **Larger shops (30+ people).** The feature breadth actually pays off.

## Where they lose

- **Buyers with a specific workflow.** Owner-operators with a strong "we do it *this* way" pattern.
- **Buyers burned by SaaS.** Anyone who's paid for two SaaS tools that got dropped by the vendor.
- **Buyers who want code ownership.** They can't offer this; it's structurally impossible.
- **Buyers under $500k revenue.** Sticker shock, plus they don't yet know what they actually need the tool to do.

## Their trajectory

- Hiring pattern (public LinkedIn): expanding sales team, expanding customer success, one senior engineering hire per quarter. Not building a moonshot — building a durable business.
- Feature velocity: consistent monthly ship. Not blistering, but steady.
- Category momentum: they're the safe pick. That safe-pick position tends to compound.

## What would change our threat rating

- **VERY HIGH** if they released a "build your workflow" module that let buyers script the state machine themselves — this would erode our workflow-fit differentiator.
- **HIGH** if they announced a "you can own your data / export to code" story — even if it's a marketing story, buyers hearing "own your data" from a big-brand competitor would pause on a custom quote.
- **LOW** if they raised prices past $499/mo without meaningful new features — that would push more of the middle market to consider a one-time custom build.

## What we do about them

- **Don't attack them by name in copy.** No comparison tables. It reads as insecurity.
- **Answer the question "how are you different from [SaaS Incumbent A]?" honestly** when a prospect asks. The honest answer wins the room.
- **When we lose to them, log the reason and stay warm.** Two of our best referral sources are former customers of theirs who eventually swapped over.
- **Watch for their AI moves.** If they ship the workflow-editor module, we amend positioning immediately.
