# Competitive Intel — Custom-CRM Build

**Type:** Example, blurred. Modelled on a real competitor scan for an operator-run custom-CRM practice serving contractors.
**Last refreshed:** 2026-06

---

## The category

Custom-build software for owner-operated contractors. The buyers we compete for are looking at three kinds of alternatives: (1) established SaaS CRMs for contractors, (2) other custom-build shops / individual developers, (3) the "do it ourselves" internal-hire route.

## Competitors, ranked by how often we hear their name

### [SaaS Incumbent A] — MODERATE threat

**What they are:** A well-funded contractor SaaS. Estimating, project management, CRM. Public pricing around $199/mo. Established brand, aggressive sales team.

**Their strength:** Feature breadth. Big library. Existing customer base means integrations with the vendor tools contractors already use.

**Their weakness:** They are a horizontal SaaS. The buyer has to bend to their workflow, not the other way around. Owner-operators who watch a demo often say "I wish I could turn X off" — but they can't.

**How they win when they win:** Bigger shops (30+ people) who need feature breadth more than workflow fit. Buyers who see monthly SaaS as normal.

**How we beat them:** Buyer owns the code, workflow follows the buyer's language not the vendor's, one-time price sits under 12 months of their subscription. **When we lose to them, it's because the buyer valued brand + support surface + integration library over workflow fit** — a legitimate call, we send them their way with a handshake.

---

### [Individual Developer / Freelancer] — LOW to MODERATE threat

**What they are:** A single developer, sometimes offshore, quoting a similar-sounding build for less.

**Their strength:** Price. Sometimes half our number.

**Their weakness:** Shipping risk. The buyer has been burned before by developers who took a deposit and went silent. Our biggest recurring intake quote: "I've paid three developers, none of them shipped anything."

**How they win when they win:** Buyers who haven't been burned yet AND are price-sensitive AND want to manage the project themselves.

**How we beat them:** The 30-day punch-list guarantee, the written scope, the deposit-vs-net-30 structure. We are more expensive on paper and cheaper in total-cost-of-shipping. **When we lose to them, it's usually a buyer who will be back in 6 months.** File the intake note and stay warm.

---

### [Adjacent Vertical Tool] — LOW threat

**What they are:** A tool that solves *part* of the buyer's problem — a scheduling app, a photo app, an estimating app. The buyer sometimes mentions them as an alternative to a full CRM.

**Their strength:** Excellent at the one thing. Buyer already uses them.

**Their weakness:** Not a CRM. The buyer eventually realizes stitching three tools together doesn't solve the problem the CRM would solve.

**How they win when they win:** Buyers who don't actually need a CRM yet. Solo operators who can hold state in their head plus a shared calendar.

**How we beat them:** Don't try to. If the buyer isn't ready for a CRM, they're not a customer. See `customers/example-not-a-fit.md` on the "not ready yet" bucket. **When we lose to them, we don't lose — we defer.**

---

### [Hire an in-house developer] — LOW threat, HIGH-signal signal

**What they are:** The buyer's decision to hire a full-time developer instead of buying a build from us.

**Their strength:** Full ownership of the team. Ongoing capacity.

**Their weakness:** Salary + benefits + management overhead + the developer's own ramp time. Most owner-operators don't want to manage a developer and can't afford one at market rates.

**How they win when they win:** Businesses past $10M in revenue who need ongoing engineering capacity, not a one-time build.

**How we beat them:** We don't try to beat them past a certain scale. **When a prospect at $8M+ says "we're thinking about hiring in-house," we tell them to hire in-house.** That answer earns more referrals than any sales pitch would.

## Pattern we watch for (not a competitor, a market signal)

A well-funded startup entering the "AI for contractor businesses" category. Every quarter or two, a new one launches with a positioning wedge that overlaps ours partially. They usually run funnel-first (paid social, standard SaaS pricing), which is a different game than warm-referral custom builds. We watch them, we don't chase them. See `deep-dive-example.md` for the pattern of what a serious threat would actually look like.

## What none of these competitors do

- Ship code the buyer owns.
- Match the buyer's exact language in the UI, screen by screen.
- Include a written 30-day guarantee that fixes bugs on us.
- Refuse pre-revenue buyers and equity deals.

**That's the differentiation, stated in facts. Not a comparison chart. Facts.**
